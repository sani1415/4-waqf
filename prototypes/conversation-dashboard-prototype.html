Conversation-Centered Dashboard Prototype

This prototype explores a hybrid future for your app: not only conversation-centered like WhatsApp, but also educationally meaningful. In this direction, each teacher-student relationship contains both a conversation layer and a dashboard layer. The dashboard is not a separate unrelated product. It is the progress lens for that specific relationship: task completion, reports percentage, results, streaks, uploads, pending review, and quick action items.

Design A: Chat + Dashboard Tabs

This is the clearest hybrid. Inside one conversation, there are visible tabs such as Chat, Dashboard, Action Needed, and maybe Docs. The dashboard is a real tab, not a separate page somewhere else in the app.

Core idea Let the user move between talking and understanding progress without leaving the relationship context.
What teacher sees in dashboard task completion percentages, overdue items, recent uploads, last result, current trend, and quick actions.
What student sees in dashboard daily completion, one-time completion, report percentage, latest teacher feedback, latest result, and next step.
Main strength Very understandable. It clearly tells the user that conversation and progress belong together.
Main risk If too many tabs are added, it can feel like a mini-dashboard app inside chat.
Decision tip: choose this if you want the safest bridge between your current app structure and your future message-centered vision.

Detailed Pro Tips

Why this is powerful It preserves the educational value of your app. Students do not lose visibility of completion percentages and reports.
Why it fits your current system Your app already has dashboard-like progress information. This design relocates it into the relationship context rather than throwing it away.
Implementation direction Use the same data you already compute for student dashboard summaries, but scope it inside the teacher-student conversation shell.
Most important warning Keep the dashboard tab summary-focused. Do not dump every record table there. Use small cards, meaningful metrics, and quick actions only.

Design B: Dashboard Sheet From Chat

In this direction, the conversation remains visually simpler. The user mostly stays on chat, and a compact dashboard opens as a slide-up sheet or side sheet when needed. So the dashboard exists, but it is not always visible as a full tab.

Core idea Make chat primary and progress secondary, but still always reachable.
Best use Good if you want stronger WhatsApp feeling while still preserving your educational progress layer.
Main strength Cleaner top navigation, less visual weight.
Main risk Some users may not open the dashboard sheet often enough unless the entry point is very obvious.
Decision tip: choose this if you want chat to feel emotionally dominant, but do not want to lose reports, percentages, and progress visibility.

Detailed Pro Tips

Product advantage The chat remains emotionally familiar. Users do not feel like they have entered a dashboard-heavy product.
Education advantage Dashboard still exists, so students can see their percentages and teachers can view progress without leaving the relationship.
Important requirement You need a very visible dashboard entry point, such as a persistent summary chip or a small stats strip near the top.
Best if Your long-term priority is “chat as home,” but you still want a stronger education layer than WhatsApp can provide.

Design C: Pinned Progress Cards Above Chat

This design keeps conversation and dashboard tightly merged. Instead of a separate dashboard tab or sheet, a compact group of pinned progress cards lives above the chat timeline. It acts like a mini-dashboard banner for the relationship.

Core idea Show important progress all the time without leaving chat.
Best use Good when quick visibility matters more than deep exploration.
Main strength Users immediately understand the student’s current state.
Main risk The top of the conversation can become crowded if too many cards are pinned.
Decision tip: choose this if you want progress always visible, but still want the app to feel mostly like a conversation tool.

Detailed Pro Tips

Strongest point Very quick reading. Teachers and students instantly see progress without tapping away from chat.
What to keep small Show only 3 to 4 key metrics, not full analytics. Think “snapshot,” not “report page.”
Best extra idea Let one tap on a metric open full detail. So the banner remains compact but still deepens when needed.
Best if You want motivation and progress awareness to remain constantly visible in the relationship.

Design D: Teacher Analytics / Student Progress

This version accepts that teacher and student dashboards should not be identical. The conversation remains shared, but the dashboard layer is role-specialized. Teachers see deeper analytics. Students see guided progress and encouragement.

Teacher emphasis trend, weak areas, pending review, quick teacher actions, comparison across recent periods.
Student emphasis what I completed, what is left, what improved, what to do next.
Main strength Very aligned with real role differences.
Main risk More design complexity and more product decisions to maintain.
Decision tip: choose this if you want the richest educational product and you are comfortable with stronger role-based differentiation.

Detailed Pro Tips

Why this can be excellent It uses the same relationship space, but respects that the teacher needs interpretation while the student needs motivation and next steps.
Best product lesson Teacher dashboard should answer: “What do I need to do with this student?” Student dashboard should answer: “How am I doing and what do I do next?”
Implementation caution Do not create completely different architecture. Keep the same shell, but change the emphasis inside the cards.
Best if You want a premium, thoughtful product rather than a simple clone of chat apps.

Design E: Relationship Hub

This is the biggest idea. The conversation does not only have chat and dashboard. The whole teacher-student relationship becomes a hub containing Chat, Dashboard, Tasks, Docs, and Results. The key is that they all remain inside the same relationship shell.

Core idea Not “separate modules of the app,” but one relationship center with multiple lenses.
Main strength Very complete. Almost everything important lives in one relationship workspace.
Main risk This is the heaviest model. If not designed carefully, it can feel like too much.
When it makes sense If your final product truly becomes relationship-centered rather than page-centered.
Decision tip: choose this if your ambition is very large and you want the relationship itself to be the main unit of the app.

Detailed Pro Tips

Why this is ambitious It turns the relationship into the product center. Users no longer think “which page should I go to?” They think “which student or teacher relationship am I working in?”
Why it may be right for you Your features already revolve around teacher-student interaction: tasks, messages, documents, reports, results, review. This model brings them together intentionally.
Big warning Keep summary information compact. Full records, full tables, and long histories should still open deeper secondary views when needed.
Best if You want the app to feel like a complete relationship workspace, not just a dashboard plus chat.
